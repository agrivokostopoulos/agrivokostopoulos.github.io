Aristeidis Grivokostopoulos

Why do some fiscal crises break patronage networks while others leave them intact? When do political institutions shape financial outcomes, and when do markets ignore politics entirely?

I study sovereign debt and political institutions across the first age of globalisation, when international capital flows reshaped what it meant to be a creditworthy state. My work asks how peripheral borrowers navigated access to London's capital markets, how parliamentary coalitions formed and dissolved under fiscal pressure, and what happens when models developed in one setting travel to another.

About

Working Paper

Made in Greece or Echoed from Europe?

Sentiment and Awareness Among International Partners During the 1931–1932 Greek Sovereign Debt Crisis

How did international perceptions of Greece shift in the months before the 1932 default? Using a novel LLM-based text-as-data approach on the Tsouderos Archive — private correspondence and memoranda of the Bank of Greece Governor — I map sentiment and topic trends in communications with foreign officials. The analysis reveals a clear shift towards pessimism from June 1931, preceding and intensifying after the Sterling Crisis of September 1931, even against heightened anxieties in the Greek press. An SVAR model shows a significant negative correlation between archive sentiment and Greek government bond yields. Change point analysis identifies two structural breaks (June and September 1931) and a shift in topic composition in November 1931 towards debt relief and financial assistance.

Working Paper

From War Finance to Fiscal Supervision

Seniority Structures and Debt Restructuring in the Ottoman Empire, Egypt, and Greece, 1870–1907

How did post-conflict fiscal institutions reshape sovereign debt markets? Using bond-level data from the London Stock Exchange, I construct current yield series for senior and junior tranches under the Ottoman Public Debt Administration (1881), the Egyptian Caisse de la Dette (1876/1882), and the Greek International Financial Commission (1898), and estimate the effect of supervision on junior-senior yield spreads via difference-in-differences. Supervision produced statistically significant spread convergence in all three cases: −1.39 pp (Ottoman), −0.42 pp (Egypt), −0.36 pp (Greece), all p<0.001. The Ottoman and Egyptian cases are price-driven, consistent with markets pricing an exit from the default zone. The Greek case diverges: legacy bondholders received variable coupons tied to revenue performance and the drachma-gold exchange rate, while new bonds carried fixed coupons. This asymmetry produced a yield spread inversion — junior trading below senior (p=0.035) — absent in the other cases. I propose a minimal extension to the Ho and Ritschl (2024) seniority reversal model, replacing fixed obligations with output-contingent payment functions, which resolves the Greek anomalies while nesting the other two cases.

Working Paper

Permeable Patronage

Fiscal Crises and Domain-Specific Defection in the Greek Parliament, 1875–1905

Does a discrete fiscal shock produce the programmatic transition that standard accounts of clientelism imply? Using 755 nominal roll call votes (132,246 MP–vote observations) covering 1,049 MPs, linked to a biographical registry and classified by policy domain, I test this across eight political shocks. Fiscal crises do not produce a programmatic transition. After the 1893 sovereign default, Peloponnesian MPs defect 7.3 percentage points more on taxation votes (p = 0.006), with no differential defection on non-fiscal votes. After Trikoupis's death in 1896, defection shifts to budget allocation. The paper decomposes legislative cohesion into observable cross-domain consistency (π-pattern) and the compliance mechanism (π-binding), adapting Krehbiel's identification problem to clientelist legislatures.

Working Paper

Between Civilisation and Constitution

Greek Parliamentary Debates on Gold Standard Adoption, 1875–1885

Why did technically correct parliamentary critics lose every vote on gold standard adoption? Using newly digitised proceedings of the Hellenic Parliament, I argue that the gold standard operated as a model — in Mary Morgan's sense — whose authority depended on narrative embedding as much as technical demonstration. Critics identified fiscal fragility, distributional burden, premature convertibility, and constitutional impropriety with striking accuracy. They lost because they lacked a counter-model. The paper's central claim is one of misapplication: the gold standard was not inherently flawed but was applied where its preconditions did not hold. A table mapping eleven key parliamentarians across constitutional, distributional, regional, and technical registers illustrates the structural fragmentation that prevented locally valid objections from coalescing. The paper also examines the absence of bimetallism — the most prominent available alternative — as a counter-narrative in the Greek debates.

Working Paper

Sterling by Design

Contractual Standardisation and Currency Dominance on the London Stock Exchange, 1869–1929

Sterling's dominance in international finance was actively constructed through contractual standardisation, not passively inherited. Using a security-level panel of 957 sovereign debt instruments from the Investors' Monthly Manual (1869–1929), I show that sterling's share of income flows rose from 79% to 99% as multi-currency clauses, bullion clauses, and non-sterling denominations were systematically eliminated. Structural break tests place the contractual cleanup in the late 1870s and 1880s, preceding periphery gold adoption. Captive borrowers without alternative capital market access paid a yield premium of 0.90 percentage points over borrowers with outside options.

Why Did Depositors Run?

Withdrawals in the German Banking Crisis of 1931 (with Philip Fliers)

Why were bank runs so pervasive in 1931, and why did depositors withdraw heavily from banks that survived? Using bimonthly balance sheet data from 1925–1935, we show that severe deposit withdrawals were widespread, highly predictable up to two years before the crisis, and differed systematically across maturities. Withdrawals were accompanied by substantial reallocation of deposits across banks.

Whatever Happened to Social History?

Social History in the Work of the Economic History Society, 1926–2026

How has social history persisted within the Economic History Society across its first century? Drawing on systematic analysis of the Economic History Review, Council records, conference programmes, and Tawney Lectures, I trace how social history underwent methodological transformation while thematic overlap persisted. Social history was never expelled, it changed methods.
