Aristeidis Grivokostopoulos

Why do some fiscal crises break patronage networks while others leave them intact? When do political institutions shape financial outcomes, and when do markets ignore politics entirely?

I study sovereign debt and political institutions across the first age of globalisation, when international capital flows reshaped what it meant to be a creditworthy state. My work asks how peripheral borrowers navigated access to London's capital markets, how parliamentary coalitions formed and dissolved under fiscal pressure, and what happens when models developed in one setting travel to another.

About

I am a second-year PhD student in the Department of Economic History at the London School of Economics, funded by the ESRC. My supervisors are Albrecht Ritschl and Olivier Accominotti.

My research examines sovereign debt, financial markets, and political institutions in 19th- and 20th-century Europe, with a focus on how fiscal crises reshape legislative coalitions and borrowing costs. I combine archival research with computational text analysis, network methods, and panel econometrics. Before academia, I worked in venture capital (Creandum, Speedinvest) and operations (Clausematch, Genie).

From War Finance to Fiscal Supervision

Seniority Structures and Debt Restructuring in the Ottoman Empire, Egypt, and Greece, 1870–1907

How did post-conflict fiscal institutions reshape sovereign debt markets? Using bond-level data from the London Stock Exchange, I compare senior-junior yield spreads under the OPDA, Caisse de la Dette, and International Financial Commission. The Greek case shows yield inversion, a state-contingent debt mechanism that existing seniority frameworks cannot explain.

Fiscal Shocks and Coalition Binding

Evidence from Greek Parliamentary Roll Calls, 1875–1905

Do fiscal shocks force clientelist legislatures to become programmatic, or do patronage networks survive even as resources collapse? Using new roll call data, I show that fiscal crises broke patronage networks in 1885 and 1897 but never replaced them with programmatic organization. Cross-topic voting consistency reached 0.80, yet defectors switched simultaneously across all domains, a patronage signature, not ideological realignment.

Between Civilisation and Constitution

Greek Parliamentary Debates on Gold Standard Adoption, 1875–1885

Why did technically correct parliamentary critics lose every vote on gold standard adoption? Using newly digitised proceedings, I argue that the gold standard operated as a "model" (in Mary Morgan's sense) whose authority depended on narrative embedding. Critics had arguments but lacked a counter-model.

Working Paper

Sterling by Design

Contractual Standardisation and Currency Dominance on the London Stock Exchange, 1869–1929

Sterling's dominance was actively constructed through contractual standardisation, not passively inherited. Using a panel of 957 sovereign debt instruments from the Investors' Monthly Manual, I show that sterling's share of income flows rose from 79% to 99% as multi-currency clauses were systematically eliminated. Structural break tests place the contractual cleanup in the late 1870s, preceding periphery gold adoption. Captive borrowers paid a 0.90 pp yield premium.

Why Did Depositors Run?

Withdrawals in the German Banking Crisis of 1931 (with Philip Fliers)

Why were bank runs so pervasive in 1931, and why did depositors withdraw heavily from banks that survived? Using bimonthly balance sheet data from 1925–1935, we show that severe deposit withdrawals were widespread, highly predictable up to two years before the crisis, and differed systematically across maturities. Withdrawals were accompanied by substantial reallocation of deposits across banks.

Whatever Happened to Social History?

Social History in the Work of the Economic History Society, 1926–2026

How has social history persisted within the Economic History Society across its first century? Drawing on systematic analysis of the Economic History Review, Council records, conference programmes, and Tawney Lectures, I trace how social history underwent methodological transformation while thematic overlap persisted. Social history was never expelled, it changed methods.
