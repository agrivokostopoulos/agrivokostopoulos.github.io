Aristeidis Grivokostopoulos

I study the political economy of sovereign debt and monetary regimes in 19th-century Europe.

My research combines archival history with computational text analysis (NLP) to understand how political narratives drive financial outcomes.

About

I am a second-year PhD student in the Department of Economic History at the London School of Economics, funded by the ESRC. My supervisors are Albrecht Ritschl and Olivier Accominotti.

My current work revisits the classical adoption of the Gold Standard in the European periphery. Before returning to academia, I worked in venture capital (Creandum, Speedinvest) and operations (Clausematch, Genie). I leverage this technical background to apply large language models and machine learning to historical parliamentary records.

Working Paper

LLM Sentiment from Diplomatic Archives

Tracking International Perceptions Before Greece's 1932 Default

How did international sentiment shift before Greece's sovereign default? I use LLM-based classification on multilingual archival documents to construct daily sentiment series linked to financial indicators via structural VAR, validated across multiple models.

Working Paper

The Thin Film of Gold in the European Periphery

Gold Standard Credibility and Greek Sovereign Debt, 1880–1914

Testing the "good housekeeping" vs. "thin film" hypotheses using newly compiled monthly bond spreads. I find that political instability and fiscal crises were the primary drivers of risk premiums, rather than monetary regime adoption per se.

Working Paper

Words and Votes in the Greek Parliament, 1865–1915

A Discursive-Network Theory of Legislative Order

How do legislative coalitions form without stable parties? I use a newly digitised corpus of parliamentary debates to map the co-evolution of rhetorical communities and voting networks in late 19th-century Greece.
