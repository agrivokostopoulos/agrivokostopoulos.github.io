I study the political economy of sovereign debt and monetary regimes in 19th-century Europe.

Between Civilisation and Constitution

Greek Parliamentary Debates on Gold Standard Adoption, 1875–1885

Why did technically correct parliamentary critics lose every vote on gold standard adoption? Using newly digitised proceedings, I argue that the gold standard operated as a "model" (in Mary Morgan's sense) whose authority depended on narrative embedding. Critics had arguments but lacked a counter-model.

Working Paper

Sterling by Design

Contractual Standardisation and Currency Dominance on the London Stock Exchange, 1869–1929

Sterling's dominance was actively constructed through contractual standardisation, not passively inherited. Using a panel of 957 sovereign debt instruments from the Investors' Monthly Manual, I show that sterling's share of income flows rose from 79% to 99% as multi-currency clauses were systematically eliminated. Structural break tests place the contractual cleanup in the late 1870s, preceding periphery gold adoption. Captive borrowers paid a 0.90 pp yield premium.

Working Paper

Why Did Depositors Run?

Withdrawals in the German Banking Crisis of 1931 (with Philip Fliers)

Why were bank runs so pervasive in 1931, and why did depositors withdraw heavily from banks that survived? Using bimonthly balance sheet data from 1925–1935, we show that severe deposit withdrawals were widespread, highly predictable up to two years before the crisis, and differed systematically across maturities. Withdrawals were accompanied by substantial reallocation of deposits across banks.
